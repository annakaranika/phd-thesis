\subsection{Conclusion}
\label{cic:sec:conclusion}
%Smart home research and practice has generally operated under the idea that users prefer their smart home control experience to be as unified as possible. Yet, as vendors continue to market their products as compatible with central control, and as smart home research, specifically Smart home operating systems research, continues to investigate new manners of unifying smart home devices, uncertainty remains about what users actually want. In this paper, we conduct a survey and follow-up interview study with smart home device owners, focused on understanding and eliciting their preferences and perceptions of smart home control schemes. We also investigate how these preferences and perceptions shape their smart home troubleshooting experiences and expectations. We find that user preferences for smart home control are context-dependent, and can differ within a single smart home based on factors such as user proximity to the device, the granularity of control the user desires, and whether they want to control one device or multiple. We also reveal current strategies for user troubleshooting of smart devices, and how smart home control schemes factor into these strategies. This work is an important first step in identifying the landscape of user preferences, expectations, and practices. By using the findings from this paper, both smart home researchers and industry practitioners can move away from conventional wisdom, and take a more \textit{user-centered} approach in their design. 

In this paper, we present an investigation into how and why smart home users navigate the spectrum of control schemes, ranging from physical device interaction to proxy-based central control, based on context, goals, and device constraints. Our survey and follow-up interviews show that users continuously adapt their smart home control strategies, as well as their smart home setups and future device procurement strategies, as they experience different levels of smart home control. Rather than treating smart home control as a static preference, our work highlights how it is a dynamic, reflective, and often labor-intensive process. Our findings suggest that users actively weigh tradeoffs between convenience, reliability, precision, and cognitive load when choosing how to control their devices. Our work emphasizes that  \textit{future systems should not only support multiple control schemes, but also actively (perhaps proactively) guide the user's movement across them}. Similarly, researchers should also reframe their evaluation metrics and system assumptions of smart home control to reflect the temporal and spatial heterogeneity of real-world usage. Via a deep dive into the user perspective of smart home control, our work is an important initial step towards moving home management systems from being {device-centric} towards becoming truly user-centric.
